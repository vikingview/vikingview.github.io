The Lord of the Rings: A Plague on Modern Fantasy
02/29/2021
Alisa Arkhangelskaya, Grade 12

“It simply isn't an adventure worth telling if there aren't any dragons”

J. R. R. Tolkien is a legendary writer whose works paved the way for modern fantasy as we know it. The Lord of the Rings series weaves together a myriad of narrations into the plot, entwining story within story creating a world--a universe. Since the fifties, Tolkien’s world became a cornerstone for the fantasy genre, influencing literature, movies and illustration. The Lord of the Rings became a powerhouse of pop culture. The series was published between 1954 to 1955, and soon after ‘Tolkien’ and ‘fantasy’ became so interchangeable, the series left a sizable imprint on society.

But which famous novelists have been influenced by the penned stories of Tolkien? In light of the achievement of The Hobbit and The Lord of the Rings in the 1960s, publishers were quick to jump on the bandwagon and meet the growing demand for fantasy novels. Some early authors acknowledged their inspiration from the series such as David Eddings, Stephen R. Donaldson and Dennis L. McKiernan. Furthermore, Gary Gygax--the creator of dungeons and dragons--recognized his attachment to the series and its influence on his revolutionary board game.

Interestingly, many of the archetypes created by J. R. R. Tolkien permeated into the beloved fantasy series of Harry Potter. Although not explicitly mentioned, J. K. Rowling’s world revolves around many of the same principles. What would happen to Harry Potter without Tolkien? It might’ve still existed though Voldemort likely would not have been the “Dark Lord”, neither would the significant Tolkien-esque plot and characterization elements. In both Harry Potter and LoTR there is a wise wizard that seems all-knowing: Gandalf and Dumbledore, a loser best friend overshadowed by the protagonist: Ron Weasley and Samwise Gamgee, cloaked dark forces, and the entire trope of the protagonist hurting when entering close proximity to the “evil”. Of course, writing an original fantasy novel devoid of Tolkien’s influence is similar to navigating a minefield and therefore extremely difficult. Even the definition of fantasy according to Oxford Languages is “a genre of imaginative fiction involving magic and adventure, especially in a setting other than the real world”.

As much as Tolkien's brilliance laboured unique results, many of the imitators who came after certainly couldn’t escape the fruitful industry standard that assimilated Northern European legend--the alvar (elves), dweoger (dwarves), vargr, and goblins--into vast fantasy worlds. With the legacy of Tolkien’s foundational texts in the fantasy genre, is the future of fantasy destined to fall back into the paradigm of The Lord of the Rings forever?
